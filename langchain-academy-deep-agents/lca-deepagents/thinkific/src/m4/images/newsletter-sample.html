This Week in Music

From arena-shaking rock anthems to reggaeton royalty meeting actual royalty, this week's releases and moments prove that great music knows no boundaries — here's what's moving the needle across our top genres.

Rock

Rock's summer heat continues to build with a stellar week of releases and landmark moments. Olivia Rodrigo dominates with her third album You Seem Pretty Sad for a Girl So in Love, a bold dive into '80s new wave and synthpop territory featuring an unmissable duet with The Cure's Robert Smith on "What's Wrong With Me"—Smith himself praised her as "genuinely fantastic as a songwriter and performer." Meanwhile, Deep Purple emerges from the classic rock vault with "Diablo," the latest single from their upcoming album SPLAT!, featuring a surprising guitar collaboration with Keith Urban. The band launches a European tour this month. Sublime returns with Until the Sun Explodes featuring Jakob Nowell, son of the original frontman, while Goose delivers their most spacious collection yet—15 tracks spanning heartland rock piano ballads to synth-driven energy. On the festival circuit, Linkin Park with new vocalist Emily Armstrong make history as Download Festival's first female-fronted headliner, signaling a shifting landscape. The Bobby Lees bring punk vitality on their Epitaph debut New Self, and Big Nothing continue cementing their status with hook-laden power-pop mastery. It's a week proving rock thrives across all its incarnations.

Latin

Latin music is buzzing with landmark moments this week. Bad Bunny dominated headlines after his private encounter with Pope Leo in Madrid—a meeting that became the highlight of the pontiff's first European visit, as the reggaeton megastar drew crowds that rivaled the papal event itself. Bad Bunny's cultural impact continues to soar; he recently became the first Latin act to top the Billboard Canadian Hot 100 and Canadian Albums charts simultaneously following his Super Bowl Halftime Show performance.

Legendary Cuban jazz trumpeter Arturo Sandoval just released his vibrant new album Sangú (Spanish for "sounds good!"), packed with the free-spirited energy he's cultivated since leaving Cuba. The 10-time Grammy winner is celebrating by playing Coachella alongside Karol G, merging generations of Latin artistry. Meanwhile, LA's Grand Performances festival kicks off its 40th season with the Latin hip-hop funk powerhouse Ozomatli on June 13, followed by the Chicano comedy troupe Culture Clash. Cypress Hill's Sen Dog also made waves discussing his new documentary Mi Familia, exploring his Cuban heritage and pioneering Latino presence in hip-hop. A poignant tribute arrived as Argentine rock legend Carlos "Indio" Solari was mourned by hundreds of thousands—his influence on Latin rock remains undeniable.

Metal

Khemmis just delivered one of the year's most compelling heavy metal statements with their self-titled album—their first studio record since 2021's Deceiver. The Colorado legends blend towering riffs, haunting melodies, and crushing emotional weight into a powerful reaffirmation of everything that's made them revered across eleven years. The album standout "Gilded Chambers" landed with a striking music video that captures the band's reinvigorated joy in simply playing loud, heavy music together.

Elsewhere in the underground, Self Loather, a Leipzig-based blackened deathcore quartet, dropped the visceral single "Upon the Dismal Shores of Acheron," showcasing the darker end of the spectrum. Meanwhile, Electric Callboy turned heads collaborating with The Offspring's Dexter Holland on "Let the Good Times Roll"—a track featuring a wildly entertaining music video that includes comedian Howie Mandel and Brian Posehn alongside metal mascot John Goblikon, arriving from their upcoming August album TANZNEID.

The genre reached a historic milestone as Linkin Park, fronted by Emily Armstrong, became Download Festival's first female-fronted headliner, marking significant progress for diversity in metal's biggest stages.

Alternative & Punk

The Bobby Lees are back with New Self, their fourth album and exciting Epitaph debut that captures the band at their most assured. Opening with the defiant title track—"I'm older now / I've gotten help / I wish you could meet my new self"—the record pairs raw punk energy with newfound confidence, from the seething Napoleon to a surprisingly sharp cover of PJ Harvey's 50ft Queenie. Elsewhere, MAN LOOKING MAN dropped their genre-bending self-titled debut this week via Octopus Rising, a project five years in the making that blends Mussorgsky and Botch influences into something beautifully uncompromising. Meanwhile, The Paradox are making serious waves: the first all-Black band to hit No. 1 on the Billboard Alternative Airplay chart with "Get the Message," they've become ambassadors for bringing rock and roll back to its roots with their infectious, humor-filled pop-punk. On the alternative front, Olivia Rodrigo trades angst for new wave glamour on you seem pretty sad for a girl so in love, featuring a stunning Robert Smith collaboration and nods to The Cure and Gary Numan that feel genuinely earned.
